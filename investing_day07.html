ğŸ‡ The Jockey and the Horse

In investing, the "Horse" is the business (the moat, the industry) and the "Jockey" is the management. A great horse can win even with a mediocre jockey, but a great jockey can turn a good horse into a champion.

ğŸ’¡ The Job of a CEO

Most CEOs focus on operations (making more widgets). But the best CEOs also focus on Capital Allocationâ€”deciding what to do with the profits the business generates.

ğŸ’° The 5 Choices of Capital Allocation

Management has exactly five options for every dollar of excess cash. Their choices reveal their competence:

  1. Reinvest in the Core: Spend on R&D or expansion. This is the best use if returns are high.
  2. Acquire Other Businesses: Can be great, but often leads to overpaying. Look for a track record of smart acquisitions.
  3. Pay Down Debt: Strengthens the balance sheet. Especially important if interest rates are high.
  4. Pay Dividends: Returning cash to shareholders. Shows the company is generating real cash.
  5. Buy Back Stock: Only a good idea if the stock is undervalued. It increases every remaining shareholder's piece of the pie.

ğŸ§¬ Incentives & Skin in the Game

Charlie Munger famously said, "Show me the incentive and I'll show you the outcome."

Insider Ownership

Does the CEO own a lot of stock? If they own millions of dollars of their own stock, they are more likely to think like an owner (you!) rather than just an employee.

Compensation Structure

Are bonuses based on stock price (short-term) or on Return on Invested Capital (long-term)? We want leaders focused on long-term value creation.

ğŸ“– Evaluating Transparency

Don't just listen to the slick presentations. Read the Annual Letter to Shareholders.

  • Do they admit mistakes? A CEO who never admits a mistake is a red flag.
  • Is it clear or filled with jargon? If they can't explain the business simply, they might be hiding something.
  • Do they focus on long-term goals or quarterly earnings? Avoid those obsessed with beating "analyst expectations" every 90 days.
