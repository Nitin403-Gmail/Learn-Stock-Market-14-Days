‚ö†Ô∏è The Truth About Trading Losses

"The first rule of trading is: Don't lose money. The second rule is: Don't forget rule number one." ‚Äî Warren Buffett (adapted)

This is the most important lesson in the entire course. You can have a winning strategy, perfect entries, and great market analysis‚Äîbut without proper risk management, you will eventually blow up your account.

‚ö†Ô∏è The Math of Losing

Look at how much you need to gain back after a loss:

LossGain Needed to Recover
10%11%
20%25%
30%43%
50%100%
75%300%
90%900%

If you lose 50% of your account, you need to make 100% just to get back to breakeven. This is why capital preservation comes first.

üìè The 1-2% Rule

The golden rule of risk management: Never risk more than 1-2% of your total account on any single trade.

Why 1-2%?

  • Survival: Even 10 losing trades in a row only costs you 10-20%
  • Psychology: Small losses are easy to accept emotionally
  • Recovery: You can recover from a 10% drawdown easily
  • Longevity: Allows you to stay in the game long enough to learn

üìä Example: 1% Risk Calculation

Account Size: $10,000
Risk Percentage: 1%
Maximum Risk per Trade: $100

This means if your stop-loss gets hit, you lose at most $100. Not a penny more. This $100 determines your position size.

üí° Risk First, Position Size Second

Most beginners think: "How many shares can I afford?"
Professionals think: "How much can I lose? What position size allows that?"

Always start with your maximum risk, then calculate your position size.

üìê Position Sizing Formula

Once you know your maximum risk, calculate your position size:

üìä Position Size Formula

Position Size = Risk Amount √∑ (Entry Price - Stop Loss Price)

Real Example

üìä Position Sizing Example

Account Size: $10,000
Risk per Trade (1%): $100
Entry Price: $50.00
Stop Loss: $48.00
Risk per Share: $2.00
Position Size: $100 √∑ $2 = 50 shares

With 50 shares, if price drops from $50 to $48 (your stop), you lose exactly $100 (1% of account). Not a penny more.

üéØ Where to Place Stop-Losses

Your stop-loss placement determines your risk per share. Here are the rules:

Rule 1: Place Stops at Technical Levels

  • Below support for long trades
  • Above resistance for short trades
  • Below/above recent swing lows/highs
  • Below/above key moving averages

Rule 2: Give Your Trade Room to Breathe

Don't place stops so tight that normal price fluctuation stops you out. Add a buffer:

  • Place stop slightly below the support level, not exactly on it
  • Use the ATR (Average True Range) indicator to measure normal volatility
  • Generally, 1-2 ATR below support works well

Rule 3: Never Move Your Stop-Loss Backward

Once set, you may move your stop in profit direction (trailing), but NEVER move it further away to "give the trade more room." That's how small losses become big losses.

‚ö†Ô∏è Stop-Loss Mistakes That Kill Accounts

  1. No stop-loss: "It will come back" - famous last words
  2. Moving stop backward: Hoping the trade recovers
  3. Stop too tight: Getting stopped out by normal volatility
  4. Stop at even numbers: Everyone puts stops there, market makers hunt them
  5. Mental stops: "I'll exit if it hits $48" - you won't

‚öñÔ∏è Risk-to-Reward Ratio

Every trade should have a favorable risk-to-reward ratio. Aim for at least 1:2 (risk $1 to make $2).

Risk:Reward Win Rate Needed to Break Even Assessment
1:1 50% Poor - not worth it
1:2 33% Good - minimum target
1:3 25% Excellent
1:5 17% Outstanding

üí° The Professional's Edge

With a 1:3 risk-reward ratio, you only need to win 25% of trades to break even. Win 40%? You're profitable. This is how professionals can be wrong more than they're right and still make money.

Only take trades where your target is at least 2x your stop distance.
