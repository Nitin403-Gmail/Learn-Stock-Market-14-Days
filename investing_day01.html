Albert Einstein is often credited with saying, "Compound interest is the eighth wonder of the world. He who understands it, earns it... he who doesn't... pays it."

What is Compounding?

Compounding is the process where your investment returns start to generate their own returns. It's the snowball effect: your money earns interest, and then that interest earns interest, and so on. Over a long period, this creates exponential growth.

ğŸ’¡ The Power of Time

Imagine you invest $1,000 and it earns 10% per year.
After 1 year, you have $1,100.
After year 2, you earn 10% on $1,100, so you have $1,210.
After 10 years, you have $2,594.
After 30 years, you have $17,449.
The magic isn't the 10% return; it's the 30-year time horizon.

This is the single most important concept for a long-term investor. Your greatest asset is not the money you start with, but the time you have.

ğŸ§  Investing vs. Speculating

"An investment operation is one which, upon thorough analysis promises safety of principal and an adequate return. Operations not meeting these requirements are speculative." â€” Benjamin Graham, The Intelligent Investor

It is critical to understand the difference between investing and speculating.

Key Differences

  • Investor's Mindset: "I am buying a piece of this company with the expectation that it will grow and become more valuable over the next 10-20 years."
  • Speculator's Mindset: "I am buying this stock because I think it will go up in the next few days/weeks, and I can sell it to someone else for a quick profit."

ğŸ’¡ Our Approach

This course is 100% focused on investing. We will learn to analyze businesses, not predict short-term price movements. We are business analysts, not fortune tellers. This approach is more reliable, less stressful, and has a much higher probability of long-term success.

ğŸ¢ Think Like a Business Owner

When you buy a share of a company like Apple (AAPL), you are not just buying a ticker symbol. You are buying a fractional ownership stake in a real business.

Your New Mindset

You are now a part-owner of a global enterprise. You have a claim on its assets, its earnings, and its future growth. Would you buy an entire local business without understanding what it does, who its customers are, or how it makes money? Of course not. You must apply the same diligence when buying stocks.

  • Don't get distracted by daily price swings. The price of a stock can be volatile, but the value of a great business is far more stable.
  • Focus on the long-term health of the business. Is it growing? Is it profitable? Does it have a strong brand?
  • Your goal is to partner with excellent companies for the long run.
