ğŸ“ˆ Profitability Ratios: How Well Does It Grow?

Profitability ratios measure how efficiently a company uses its resources to generate profit. For long-term investors, sustainability is key.

ğŸ’¡ Return on Equity (ROE)

Formula: Net Income / Shareholder's Equity

ROE tells you how much profit the company generates for every dollar of shareholder equity. A high ROE (generally >15-20%) indicates a business that is effectively compounding its own capital.

ğŸ’¡ Return on Capital Employed (ROCE)

Formula: EBIT / (Total Assets - Current Liabilities)

ROCE is often considered superior to ROE because it includes debt in the calculation. It tells you how well the company uses all its capital (both equity and debt) to generate earnings. Look for ROCE that is consistently higher than the company's cost of borrowing.

ğŸ” Operating Efficiency: The Margins

Margins tell you how much of every sales dollar actually makes it to the bottom line.

  • Operating Margin: (Operating Income / Revenue). This shows how well the core business is running before taxes and interest.
  • Net Margin: (Net Income / Revenue). The final percentage of profit.

ğŸŒŸ The Trend is Your Friend

Individual numbers are less important than the trend. Is the operating margin expanding over the last 5 years? That's a sign of a strengthening competitive advantage or better efficiency.

ğŸ›¡ï¸ Financial Health & Solvency

A company can be profitable but still "fragile" if it has too much debt. We use health ratios to ensure the company won't go bust during a recession.

1. Debt-to-Equity (D/E) Ratio

Shows the proportion of equity and debt used to finance assets. Ideally, for conservative long-term investing, you want a D/E ratio under 1.0, though this varies by industry (utilities have higher debt, tech has lower).

2. Current Ratio (Liquidity)

Formula: Current Assets / Current Liabilities

Can the company pay its short-term bills? A ratio above 1.5 is generally comfortable. Anything below 1.0 is a red flagâ€”the company might need to raise cash or take on expensive debt soon.
