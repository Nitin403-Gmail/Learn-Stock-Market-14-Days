Valuation Ratios: Is the Price Right?

üéØ Today's Learning Objectives

  1. Understand that "Price" and "Value" are two different things.
  2. Master the Price-to-Earnings (P/E) ratio and its limitations.
  3. Learn about P/S, P/B, and PEG ratios for different types of companies.
  4. Recognize "Value Traps": Why a "cheap" stock can stay cheap forever.

üí∞ Price vs. Value

"Price is what you pay. Value is what you get." ‚Äî Warren Buffett

As a long-term investor, your goal is to buy businesses for less than they are worth. Valuation ratios help us determine if a stock is overvalued (expensive) or undervalued (a bargain).

üìà The Price-to-Earnings (P/E) Ratio

This is the most common valuation metric. It tells you how much the market is willing to pay for every dollar of the company's profit.

üí° Formula: Share Price / Earnings Per Share (EPS)

If a stock is $100 and earns $5 per year, its P/E is 20. This means you are paying 20x the current earnings to own the company.

Interpreting P/E:

  • High P/E: Investors expect high growth in the future. (Common in Tech)
  • Low P/E: Investors expect slow growth or see potential risks. (Common in Utilities or Banks)
  • Trailing P/E: Based on the last 12 months of actual earnings.
  • Forward P/E: Based on analysts' estimates of next year's earnings.

üõ† Other Essential Tools

1. Price-to-Sales (P/S) Ratio

Used for companies that aren't profitable yet (like high-growth startups). It compares price to the total revenue generated.

2. Price-to-Book (P/B) Ratio

Compares market price to the company's net asset value. Crucial for banking and insurance companies.

3. PEG Ratio (Price/Earnings to Growth)

A higher P/E might be justified if the company is growing very fast. The PEG ratio divides the P/E by the growth rate. A PEG < 1.0 is often considered a great "growth at a reasonable price" (GARP) opportunity.

‚ö†Ô∏è Beware the Value Trap

A "Value Trap" is a stock that looks cheap (low P/E) but is actually a bad investment because the business is fundamentally declining.

üö© Red Flags of a Value Trap

  • Declining revenue year over year.
  • High debt combined with a falling share price.
  • An industry being disrupted (e.g., Blockbuster when Netflix arrived).

‚úÖ Today's Action Steps

üìù Action Step 1: Sector Comparison (15 minutes)

Find the average P/E ratio for two different sectors (e.g., Software vs. Consumer Staples).

Why is the "expensive" software group potentially a better investment than the "cheap" staple group? (Hint: Think about growth rates).

üìù Action Step 2: PEG Calculation (15 minutes)

Find a high-growth stock (like Nvidia or Amazon). Look up its Forward P/E and its projected earnings growth. Calculate the PEG. Does the growth justify the high price?

üîë Key Takeaways

  1. A low P/E doesn't always mean a stock is a bargain; a high P/E doesn't always mean it's expensive.
  2. Valuation must be viewed in the context of growth and industry averages.
  3. The PEG ratio is a powerful tool to balance price and growth.
  4. Always ask: "Why is this stock cheap? Is the business broken or just out of favor?"
