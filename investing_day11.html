üß† Your Brain is Your Worst Enemy

"The investor's chief problem‚Äîand even his worst enemy‚Äîis likely to be himself." ‚Äî Benjamin Graham

Evolution designed your brain to avoid pain and seek instant gratification. In the stock market, these biological hard-wirings cause you to buy when things are expensive (greed) and sell when they are cheap (fear).

üö´ The 4 Most Dangerous Biases

1. Loss Aversion

The pain of losing $100 is twice as strong as the joy of gaining $100. This causes investors to hold on to "losers" too long, hoping they'll break even, while selling "winners" too early to lock in small gains.

2. Confirmation Bias

We only seek out information that agrees with our current thesis. If you love a stock, you'll read bullish articles and ignore the bearish ones. Fix: Actively search for the smartest people who disagree with you.

3. Recency Bias (FOMO)

The tendency to believe what happened recently will continue to happen. "The market has been going up for 3 years, so it's safe to buy now!" This leads to buying at the top of bubbles.

4. Hindsight Bias

Thinking you knew an event would happen after it occurred. "I knew the market would crash!" No, you didn't. You're just rewriting your own history. (Recall the Decision Journal from Day 8).

üé≠ Meeting Mr. Market

Benjamin Graham introduced the analogy of Mr. Market. Imagine you own half a business with a partner named Mr. Market. Every day, he offers to buy your half or sell you his. He is a manic-depressive.

  • Some days he's ecstatic and offers a ridiculously high price.
  • Other days he's suicidal and offers a ridiculously low price.

üåü The Rational Response

Mr. Market is there to serve you, not lead you. If his price is high, sell to him. If it's low, buy from him. If he's just being noisy, ignore him. His erratic emotions don't change the underlying value of the business.

‚öñÔ∏è Strategies for Rationality

  • Check your portfolio less: Checking daily invites emotional tampering. Check monthly or quarterly instead.
  • Automate Your Investing: Use Dollar-Cost Averaging. If you buy the same amount every month, you automatically buy more shares when they are cheap and fewer when they are expensive.
  • The Sleep Test: If you can't sleep because a stock is down 10%, you either have too much money in it or you don't understand the business well enough.

‚úÖ Today's Action Steps

üìù Action Step 1: Identify Your Bias (15 minutes)

Think about your most recent investment. Did any of the 4 biases above play a role? Were you following the crowd (Recency)? Were you only reading positive news (Confirmation)? Be honest.

üìù Action Step 2: The "Anti-Thesis" (20 minutes)

Pick one company you are bullish on. Find a well-researched article or video explaining why it's a sell. Read/watch it with an open mind. Does your conviction still hold? If not, why?

üîë Key Takeaways

  1. The stock market is a transfer of wealth from the impatient/emotional to the patient/rational.
  2. Confirmation bias is the silent killer of portfolios. Always seek disconfirming evidence.
  3. Mr. Market is your servant, not your guide.
  4. Use automated systems and written rules to protect yourself from your own impulses.
