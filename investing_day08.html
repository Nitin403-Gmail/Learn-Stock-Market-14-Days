ğŸ— The Investment Thesis

An investment thesis is a short paragraph (or a few bullet points) explaining why you are buying a stock and what you expect to happen over the next 3-5 years.

ğŸ’¡ If You Can't Write It Down, You Don't Own It

Writing forces clarity. If you can't explain why a stock is a good investment in 100 words or less, your understanding is too fuzzy. This document will be your anchor when the market gets volatile.

ğŸ“ A Standard Research Framework

Use this 5-step checklist to evaluate any new stock idea:

1. Business Profile

What do they do? How do they make money? (Recall Day 2: The Business Model).

2. The Moat Analysis

What protects them? Is it Brand? Switching Costs? Network Effects? (Recall Day 6).

3. Financial Health

Check the ROE, Margins, and Debt levels. Are the numbers improving? (Recall Days 3 & 4).

4. Valuation

Is the Current P/E or PEG reasonable compared to historical averages? (Recall Day 5).

5. Management & Incentives

Does the CEO have skin in the game? Are they good capital allocators? (Recall Day 7).

ğŸ’€ The Pre-Mortem: Thinking About Failure

Most investors only focus on the "upside." Pros focus on the "downside."

ğŸ›  Perform a Pre-Mortem

Imagine it's three years from now and the investment has been a total disaster. You've lost 50% of your money. What happened?

  • Did a new competitor emerge?
  • Did the industry face new regulations?
  • Did management make a terrible acquisition?

Identifying these risks now helps you monitor them objectively in the future.

ğŸ““ The Decision Journal

Every time you buy (or sell) a stock, record:

  • The Date and Price.
  • The Investment Thesis.
  • Your current emotional state (Are you excited? Nervous?).
  • Key metrics you will watch (e.g., "Revenue must grow at 10%").

This prevents "Hindsight Bias"â€”the tendency to think you knew something all along when you actually didn't.
