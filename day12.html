üé® Why Diversification Matters

"Diversification is the only free lunch in investing." ‚Äî Harry Markowitz, Nobel Prize Winner

Diversification means spreading your investments so that no single position can destroy your portfolio. It's not about maximizing returns‚Äîit's about surviving long enough to compound.

What Diversification Does

  • Reduces volatility: When one holding drops, others may hold or rise
  • Protects against black swans: Company fraud, unexpected news, sector crashes
  • Allows you to stay in the game: No single loss knocks you out
  • Reduces emotional stress: No single position feels like "life or death"

‚ö†Ô∏è Concentration Risk Example

Imagine putting 50% of your portfolio in one stock. The company announces fraud‚Äîstock drops 80% overnight. Your portfolio just lost 40%. That's months or years of work erased in hours.

With proper diversification (5% max per position), the same event costs you only 4%. Painful but survivable.

üìè The 5% Diversification Rule

The Rule: No Single Position > 5% of Portfolio

At the time of purchase, no single stock or trade should represent more than 5% of your total portfolio value.

Example: $20,000 portfolio √ó 5% = $1,000 maximum per position

Why 5%?

  • If a position goes to zero, you lose only 5%‚Äîrecoverable
  • Allows for 20+ positions = good diversification
  • Still concentrated enough that winners make a difference
  • Industry standard for professional portfolio management

Adjustments Based on Account Size

Account Size Recommended Max Position Number of Positions
Under $5,000 10-15% 7-10
$5,000 - $25,000 5-10% 10-20
$25,000+ 3-5% 20-30

üí° Small Account Reality

With a $2,000 account, holding 20 positions of $100 each isn't practical (commissions eat you alive). Start with 5-10 positions, but still apply the 1-2% risk per trade rule. As your account grows, add diversification.

üè¢ Sector Diversification

Don't just own different stocks‚Äîown stocks in different sectors. Sectors move together, so 5 tech stocks isn't diversification.

The 11 S&P 500 Sectors

  1. Technology (AAPL, MSFT)
  2. Healthcare (JNJ, UNH)
  3. Financials (JPM, BAC)
  4. Consumer Discretionary (AMZN, TSLA)
  5. Consumer Staples (PG, KO)
  6. Energy (XOM, CVX)
  7. Industrials (CAT, UPS)
  8. Materials (LIN, SHW)
  9. Utilities (NEE, DUK)
  10. Real Estate (AMT, PLD)
  11. Communication Services (GOOGL, META)

Sector Diversification Rule

No more than 25% of your portfolio should be in any single sector.

Aim for exposure to at least 5-6 different sectors.

üîÑ Portfolio Rebalancing

Over time, winners grow and losers shrink, throwing off your diversification. Rebalancing brings it back in line.

When to Rebalance

  • Time-based: Quarterly or annually
  • Threshold-based: When any position exceeds 2x its target (e.g., 5% becomes 10%)
  • After major moves: Market crash, individual stock surge

How to Rebalance

  1. Trim positions that have grown too large
  2. Add to positions that are now underweight
  3. Or simply direct new money to underweight areas

üí° Winner's Tax Problem

Be mindful of taxes when rebalancing. Selling winners triggers capital gains taxes. Options:
‚Ä¢ Let winners run slightly overweight if tax impact is high
‚Ä¢ Direct new money to underweight areas instead of selling
‚Ä¢ Use tax-advantaged accounts (IRA, 401k) for frequent rebalancing

‚ö†Ô∏è Common Portfolio Mistakes

Mistake 1: Fake Diversification

Owning AAPL, MSFT, GOOGL, AMZN, and META isn't diversified‚Äîit's 100% tech/growth. A tech sector crash hits all of them simultaneously.

Mistake 2: Over-Diversification (Diworsification)

Owning 50+ stocks dilutes your winners. If your best idea goes up 50% but it's only 2% of your portfolio, you gain 1%. Just buy an index fund at that point.

Mistake 3: Ignoring Correlation

Some stocks move together even if they're in different sectors. Oil stocks and airline stocks (fuel cost) often move inversely. Understand how your holdings interact.

Mistake 4: No Cash Position

Being 100% invested means you can't buy the dip when opportunities arise. Keep 10-20% cash for opportunities and emergencies.

üìù Action Step 1: Portfolio Analysis (20 minutes)

If you have an existing portfolio, analyze it:

  1. What percentage is your largest position?
  2. What percentage is in your largest sector?
  3. How many different sectors are you exposed to?
  4. Do you have any cash position?

  1. Maximum position size: ____%
  2. Maximum sector allocation: ____%
  3. Number of positions: ____
  4. Cash reserve: ____%

üìù Action Step 3: Rebalancing Plan (10 minutes)

Decide on your rebalancing approach:

  1. Frequency: Quarterly / Annually / Threshold-based
  2. Trigger: When position exceeds ___% of target
  3. Method: Sell winners / Direct new cash / Both

üîë Key Takeaways

  1. Diversification protects against catastrophic losses
  2. 5% max per position (adjust for small accounts)
  3. 25% max per sector
  4. Own stocks across at least 5-6 sectors
  5. Rebalance quarterly or when positions exceed thresholds
  6. Keep 10-20% cash for opportunities
