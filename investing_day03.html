How to Read Financial Statements

‚è± 60 minutes üìö Critical Skill üéØ High-Impact Concept

üéØ Today's Learning Objectives

  1. Understand the purpose of the three main financial statements.
  2. Learn the basic components of the Income Statement (Revenue, Expenses, Net Income).
  3. Learn the basic components of the Balance Sheet (Assets, Liabilities, Equity).
  4. Learn the basic components of the Cash Flow Statement (Operating, Investing, Financing).
  5. See how these three statements work together to paint a picture of a company's health.

üìä The Big Three: A Company's Report Card

Financial statements are the language of business. As an investor, you must learn to speak this language. They tell you a story about a company's health and performance. There are three key statements to understand.

üí° An Analogy

Income Statement: Like a video of the company's performance over a period (e.g., one year). It tells you if it was profitable.
Balance Sheet: A snapshot of the company's financial health at a single point in time. It shows what it owns and what it owes.
Cash Flow Statement: Tracks the actual cash moving in and out of the company. Cash is the lifeblood of a business.

1. The Income Statement (Profit & Loss)

Question it answers: "How profitable is the company?"

Key Items:

  • Revenue (or Sales): The total amount of money the company generated from selling its products or services. This is the "top line."
  • Cost of Goods Sold (COGS): The direct costs of creating the products or services.
  • Gross Profit: Revenue - COGS.
  • Operating Expenses: Other costs to run the business (salaries, marketing, R&D).
  • Operating Income: Gross Profit - Operating Expenses. Shows the profitability of the core business operations.
  • Net Income (or Earnings/Profit): The famous "bottom line." This is the profit left over after all expenses, interest, and taxes have been paid.

üí° What to Look For

Is revenue consistently growing? Is the company profitable (positive net income)? Are its profit margins stable or increasing?

2. The Balance Sheet

Question it answers: "What is the company's financial position?"

The balance sheet is based on a fundamental equation: Assets = Liabilities + Shareholder's Equity

Key Items:

  • Assets: What the company owns (e.g., cash, inventory, property, equipment).
  • Liabilities: What the company owes to others (e.g., debt, accounts payable).
  • Shareholder's Equity: The net worth of the company. It's what would be left for shareholders if all assets were sold and all debts were paid.

üí° What to Look For

Does the company have more assets than liabilities? How much debt does it have? Is the shareholder's equity growing over time?

3. The Cash Flow Statement

Question it answers: "Where is the company's cash coming from and where is it going?"

Profit is an opinion, but cash is a fact. A company can report a profit but still go bankrupt if it runs out of cash. This statement is crucial.

Three Sections:

  • Cash Flow from Operations: Cash generated by the company's core business activities. This should almost always be a positive number for a healthy company.
  • Cash Flow from Investing: Cash used for investments, such as buying new equipment or acquiring other companies. Often negative for growing companies.
  • Cash Flow from Financing: Cash from activities like issuing or buying back stock, taking on debt, or paying dividends.

üí° What to Look For

Is the company generating strong, positive cash flow from its operations? This is the most important sign of a healthy business.

‚úÖ Today's Action Steps

üìù Action Step 1: Find a Company's Financials (15 minutes)

Go to a free financial website like Yahoo Finance, MarketWatch, or Finviz.

  1. Search for a large, well-known company (e.g., Apple - AAPL, Microsoft - MSFT).
  2. Find the "Financials" or "SEC Filings" tab.
  3. Locate the Income Statement, Balance Sheet, and Cash Flow Statement. Don't worry about understanding every line item yet. Just get familiar with where to find them and what they look like.

üìù Action Step 2: Answer Three Key Questions (10 minutes)

Using the statements you found for your chosen company, try to answer these questions for the most recent full year:

  1. Income Statement: Was the company profitable? (Look at Net Income).
  2. Balance Sheet: How much Total Debt does it have? How much Cash?
  3. Cash Flow Statement: Was its Cash Flow from Operations positive?
